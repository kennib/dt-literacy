## Learning Intentions

Students will understand that fractions represent equal parts of a whole and can identify, name, and write common fractions (halves, quarters, thirds).

## Success Criteria

- I can identify fractions shown in diagrams
- I can write fractions using numerator and denominator notation
- I can explain what the numerator and denominator represent
- I can shade a fraction of a given shape

## Assessment Strategies

Observation of student responses during whole-class discussion. Exit ticket where students shade and label three different fractions on provided shapes. Anecdotal notes on common misconceptions noted during circulation.

## Resources and Materials

- Fraction wall posters (classroom display)
- Coloured pencils
- Grid paper worksheets (differentiated — three levels)
- Interactive whiteboard with fraction manipulatives (Math Learning Centre)
- Fraction circles (physical manipulatives, one set per pair)

## Differentiation

**Extension:** Students compare and order fractions on a number line, and begin exploring equivalent fractions (e.g. 2/4 = 1/2).

**Support:** Students work with physical fraction circles only, focusing on halves and quarters. Sentence starters provided: "This shape is divided into ___ equal parts. Each part is called a ___."

**EAL/D:** Visual fraction charts available with labelled diagrams in English. Key vocabulary cards on desks throughout the lesson.

## ICT Considerations

Use the interactive fraction tool on the IWB (Math Learning Centre – Fractions app) during the introduction to visually demonstrate equal parts before distributing physical manipulatives. Students may use iPads with the 'Fractions' app during the body phase for independent practice and self-checking.

## Reflection

### What the students understood

Most students grasped the concept of equal parts and could shade halves and quarters confidently by the end of the lesson. The pizza-sharing context was a highly effective entry point — students who struggled with abstract notation were still able to demonstrate understanding through the physical manipulatives.

### What I need to revisit with the students

Several students confused numerator and denominator when writing fraction notation. I need to revisit this with a memorable anchor — possibly "denominator = down below" — and provide more structured practice before moving on to comparing fractions.

### What I would change when I next teach this lesson

Allow more time for the manipulative exploration phase. Students were highly engaged during the fraction circles activity and the transition to the written worksheet felt rushed. I would also introduce the grid paper recording earlier so students are not switching modes at the end.

### How I could develop this lesson further

**Differentiation:** Create a properly tiered worksheet with three clearly distinct levels, rather than one worksheet with extension questions added at the bottom. Each tier should have different representations, not just harder numbers.

**Technology:** Use the fraction bar interactive on the IWB during the introduction to visually demonstrate equal parts *before* handing out physical manipulatives, so students have a shared reference point.

**Assessment:** Add a brief verbal check-in mid-lesson (quick thumbs up/sideways/down) to catch misconceptions earlier, before students spend 15 minutes practising an incorrect method.
